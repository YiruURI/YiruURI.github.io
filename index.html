California Bill Removes Citizenship Requirement for Police Officers

By Yiru Liu | Lincoln High School

“I noticed kids who were really distinguishing themselves in our academy, but they weren’t eligible to be peace officers.”—Police Chief Joe Farrow

California Senate Bill 960 (SB 960) was recently passed by the California State Senate and signed into law by the Governor. This bill made several significant changes to the requirements for police officers, also known as “peace officers,” in the state of California, including the removal of citizenship requirements for police officers in the state.

Under the previous law, peace officers in California were required to either be a citizen of the United States or a permanent resident who was eligible for and had applied for citizenship. SB 960 removes this requirement and instead requires peace officers to be legally authorized to work in the United States, consistent with federal law and regulations. This change brings California in line with federal law, which allows certain non-citizens to work in the United States with the proper documentation.

According to supporters, the purpose of the new law is to bring greater diversity to the police force of California, as well as to remove a major barrier that prevented otherwise qualified individuals from becoming police officers. “Effective policing requires peace officers who are part of the communities they police,” argued the California Public Defenders Association in support of the bill. “It is also important that members of law enforcement reflect the diverse communities they serve,” the association continues.

There has been some controversy surrounding the bill, notably from right-wing viewpoints who worry that the bill would allow illegal immigrants to become police officers in the state. However, clarification from supporters reveals that although the language in the bill does not explicitly address illegal immigrants, the bill requires police officers to be legally authorized to work in the United States. “I noticed kids who were really distinguishing themselves in our academy, but they weren’t eligible to be peace officers,” said Police Chief Joe Farrow, who spearheaded the bill. “All it says is that to become a peace officer in California, you need to have a legal right to work here … All the background checks, all the checks and balances remain the same.”

A related change made by SB 960 is the removal of the prohibition on appointing non-citizens as members of the California Highway Patrol. This agency, which is part of the Transportation Agency and responsible for enforcing traffic laws and maintaining public safety on California's highways, had previously only been allowed to appoint citizens as members. With the passage of SB 960, the California Highway Patrol will now be able to consider non-citizens for appointment as well.

Opponents of the bill have raised concerns about the potential impact on public safety and the integrity of the peace officer profession. They argue that the citizenship requirement was put in place for good reason that police officers should be allegiance to the United States and the Constitution, and that removing it could potentially expose the state to security risks.  “According to the Peace Officers’ Research Association of California, ‘Although we understand the author’s intent with the bill, we have serious concerns that if SB 960 became law, non-United States citizens, who become peace officers, would have access to highly confidential information,’ ” noted the Senate Third Reading of the bill.

In addition to changing the citizenship requirement for peace officers, SB 960 also made changes to the accreditation standards for the education that peace officers must have. Previously, the minimum education requirement for peace officers was a high school diploma or the equivalent, and accreditation for educational institutions had to come from certain recognized agencies or organizations. SB 960 expands the recognized accrediting organizations to include Cognia, a non-profit organization that focuses on improving schools and education systems around the world. “This bill would revise the accreditation standards to include an organization holding full membership in Cognia,” stated the text of the bill.

Overall, the passage of SB 960 represents a significant shift in the requirements and standards for peace officers in California. By removing the requirement that police officers be either a citizen of the United States or a permanent resident who is eligible for and has applied for citizenship, the bill significantly expands the pool of candidates for police officer positions, which may open up the possibility of more individuals being able to apply. This could potentially lead to a larger pool of qualified candidates to choose from, which could be particularly beneficial in areas where there are shortages of law enforcement personnel. In addition, by removing the citizenship requirement, the bill may allow more individuals from diverse backgrounds, such as non-citizens, to become police officers. This could potentially lead to a more diverse law enforcement workforce that better reflects the communities it serves. While the bill has its supporters and detractors, it remains to be seen how these changes will impact the state’s law enforcement agencies and their respective communities.
